What gets tracked

Each text block carries a data-wb-text attribute with a stable id. The tracker watches every one of them independently: it gates on viewport visibility, scroll velocity, and active engagement, then accumulates dwell time per element. There is no "first paragraph only" — every block competes for your attention on its own.

A block counts as read once you've spent enough time with it actually in view. Blink past it while scrolling and it won't count; rest on it and it will. Reads are reported with the time spent and whether they were partial, so the server can tell a skim from a careful read.

Identical content embeds once on the server, keyed by a content hash, and is shared across every customer who saw it. The same paragraph on ten landing pages is one embedding, attributed to ten passports at query time — cheap to store, exact to recall.

Why it matters

Most analytics tell you a page was viewed. WhiteBox tells you which paragraphs a specific person actually read, in what order, and for how long — the difference between "visited the services page" and "read the implant pricing twice."

Because reads flow into the same awareness store as emails, calls, and CRM records, one question to /analytics/ask can weave them together: what a customer read, what they were told, and what they said back.

And it degrades gracefully. A read still in progress when you leave the page is flushed on unload via sendBeacon, so even a ten-second visit is captured rather than lost.

What actually happens during a root canal

Few phrases make people wince like "you need a root canal." The reputation is worse than the reality: the treatment doesn't cause the pain, it ends it. The ache you arrive with comes from an infected or inflamed nerve inside the tooth — the procedure simply removes it.

It begins the way an ordinary filling does. The area is fully numbed — most patients feel pressure but no pain — and a thin protective sheet keeps the tooth clean and dry. If you're nervous, ask about sedation; anxious patients are exactly who it's for.

"By the time you're in the chair, the worst part is already behind you. We're not adding pain — we're removing the source of it." — Dr. Lena Okafor, endodontist

The dentist opens a small access point, removes the infected pulp, then cleans and shapes the narrow canals inside the root and seals them. Many teeth are finished in a single visit; a crown often follows to protect what remains, especially on a molar that does the heavy chewing.

Recovery is usually mild — a little tenderness for a day or two, easily managed. A few things help:

  • Take over-the-counter pain relief before the numbness wears off, not after.
  • Chew on the other side until the permanent crown is placed.
  • Keep up gentle brushing and flossing — a treated tooth still needs daily care.

The point of saving the tooth is simple: nothing replaces a natural tooth quite as well as the one you already have. A dental implant is an excellent backup — but a tooth kept is a tooth that still feels like yours, and the stories patients tell us afterward are mostly relief.
